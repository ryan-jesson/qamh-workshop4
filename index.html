Cluster 1

Setting the right scene

The moves that happen before — and around — the conversation.

Vignette · Starting Cold
Scenario

Linda works in QAMH's Policy & Impact team. This morning she has three related tasks to get through: update a funding submission due Friday, draft a briefing note for her manager based on a sector report that landed yesterday, and pull together three dot points summarising QAMH's position on a consultation that just opened.

She has three documents open on her desktop: the draft submission, the sector report, and QAMH's existing position paper. She opens Claude, pastes in the draft submission, and types:

"Can you help me update this funding submission?"

Claude starts making suggestions. They're reasonable. But when Linda switches to the briefing note, she has to re-explain the context. Then again for the consultation dot points. By the end of the session she feels like she spent half her time managing Claude rather than working with it.

What would you do differently before sharing any of those documents?

Which pillar of good prompting does this connect to — and why does it matter when you have multiple tasks?

The Scene-Setting Move

Before sharing a single document, take thirty seconds to lay out the shape of the whole session — and explicitly ask the model to hold before actioning anything.

"I'm working on three things today that are all connected. I'll be sharing several documents with you shortly — a draft funding submission, a sector report, and a position paper. For now, I just want you to read them and take them in. Don't action anything yet. Once I've shared everything, I'll tell you exactly what I need from each one. Let me share those now."

Then share the documents one by one. Once they're all in, a single directing prompt does the work:

"Now — starting with the funding submission: here's what I need..."

The documents have done the context-setting themselves. Continuity is preserved across all three tasks. Claude hasn't run ahead in the wrong direction while you were still loading the picture.

The explicit hold is the part most people miss. It's what makes the rest of the move work.
Vignette · Asking for Feedback
Scenario

Chris has spent the last month developing a proposal for a new peer support program targeting rural members. He's genuinely proud of it — it's innovative, addresses a real gap, and he thinks the three-tier model is the standout feature.

He opens Claude and types:

"I've been developing a peer support program for our rural members and I think it's a really strong model — it's innovative and fills a genuine gap in the sector. Here's the proposal. Can you give me feedback?"

Claude responds:

"This is a really thoughtful and well-structured proposal! The three-tier peer support model is particularly innovative and directly addresses the rural service gap in a way that feels genuinely sector-leading. A few minor suggestions to strengthen it further..."

Chris feels validated. He makes the minor tweaks and moves forward.

What's the problem with how Chris set this up?

What might he have missed as a result — and which element of the framework does this connect to?

Strategic Withholding

Claude didn't give Chris an honest assessment. It gave him a reflection of his own enthusiasm back at him — because he told it what to think before asking it to think.

LLMs are naturally sycophantic. When you lead with your opinion, the model anchors to it. Weaknesses get softened. Risks get minimised. The feedback feels good but doesn't serve you.

The fix is to withhold the opinion entirely — share the materials, set the context, but don't tell Claude what you think before asking what it thinks.

"Here's a proposal I've been developing. Before I share my own thoughts on it, I want your honest assessment — what are the genuine strengths here, and what are the risks or weaknesses I might not be seeing?"

That single change produces a fundamentally different response. Claude is no longer confirming your view. It's forming its own.

The opinion you withhold is the bias you avoid.
Vignette · Before You Share Your Idea
Scenario

Graham needs to figure out how to respond to a difficult piece of feedback QAMH has received from a member organisation. The criticism is pointed and somewhat unfair — but the relationship matters and the response needs to be carefully calibrated.

He has a clear instinct about how to approach it: acknowledge the concern, gently reframe the context, and close by inviting further dialogue. He opens Claude and types:

"I've received some difficult feedback from a member organisation — it's a bit pointed and not entirely fair, but the relationship matters. My thinking is to acknowledge the concern, gently reframe the context, and close by inviting further dialogue. Can you help me draft a response along those lines?"

What does Graham risk by leading with his idea?

What would he gain by withholding it — and for how long?

Strategic Withholding

The moment Graham describes his approach, the conversation is anchored. Claude will evaluate his idea — refining it, improving it, building on it. But it will never escape its gravity.

The question Graham hasn't answered yet is: is his instinct the best option? He doesn't know, because he hasn't heard any alternatives.

The better move is to share the situation first — and ask Claude what it would consider before offering his own idea.

"Here's the situation: [describes the feedback and the relationship context]. I'm working out how best to respond. Before I tell you what I'm thinking, what approaches would you consider here? What are the options, and what are the trade-offs?"

Now Claude generates independently. Graham gets a genuine menu of options — some of which he may not have considered. He can then evaluate them, combine them, or decide his original instinct was right after all.

The idea you withhold is the option you haven't heard yet.
Vignette · The Weekly Meeting
Scenario

Every week, Kat meets with Chloe — her manager — to discuss the projects she's working on: priorities, blockers, new directions, decisions made. It's a rich conversation. Afterwards, Kat goes and does her work.

She often uses Claude to help with tasks that are directly connected to what came up in those meetings. But when she opens Claude, she has to re-explain the context from scratch each time — the background, the constraints, the decisions, the nuances of what was actually said.

What could you do to give Claude genuine insight into those conversations — not just a summary you've reconstructed from memory?

Why might a reconstruction from memory fall short?

Recording & Transcribing

Record the conversation. Transcribe it. Feed the raw transcript into Claude.

The difference between a reconstructed summary and a raw transcript is significant. A summary is filtered through your memory and your current framing. A transcript contains the actual language used, the specific decisions made, the texture of how things were discussed — details you wouldn't think to include and couldn't reconstruct accurately even if you tried.

"Here's the transcript from my weekly catch-up. Before we work on anything, I want you to read this so you have the real context — not just my summary of it."

This is a pre-AI habit that dramatically amplifies everything that follows. It applies to any conversation that generates context: team meetings, client calls, collaborative sessions, debrief discussions.

The richest context you can give Claude is a conversation that actually happened. Capture it.
Vignette · The Hard Prompt
Scenario

Chelsea is one of those people who can walk into a room and immediately articulate a complex situation with clarity, warmth, and nuance. In meetings, she's the one who names the thing everyone was struggling to say. Verbally, she's exceptional.

At the keyboard, it's a different story. Typing slows her down, flattens her thinking, and strips out the texture. She finds herself editing as she goes — cutting the nuance, simplifying the context, losing the thread. What ends up on screen rarely matches what was in her head.

She uses Claude regularly and genuinely finds it helpful. But she often finishes a session feeling more drained than she expected — not because the task was hard, but because getting the context in felt like hard work. Today's task involves no existing documents she can upload. What Claude needs is Chelsea's working knowledge of a situation — the history, the dynamics, the competing priorities — and that knowledge lives in her head, not on a page.

She sits down to start a prompt. She knows what she wants to say. She starts typing. Deletes. Starts again.

What might serve Chelsea better here than typing?

Is this about the tool, the task, or the person — or all three?

Speaking Rather Than Typing

Chelsea doesn't have a prompting problem. She has a medium problem.

Voice input removes the keyboard as a bottleneck between thought and context. Speaking feels natural to Chelsea in a way that typing doesn't — and the result is a richer, faster, more detailed prompt that actually matches what was in her head.

The move isn't for everyone. But for anyone who thinks faster than they type, finds editing-as-you-go strips away nuance, or regularly feels that the context they give Claude is a pale version of what they actually know — speaking is worth trying.

Open Claude. Tap the microphone. Talk to it the way you'd explain the situation to a colleague. Don't edit. Just speak.

The difference between a typed prompt and a spoken one — for the right person — is the difference between a summary and a briefing.

Play to your strengths. The medium is part of the tool.
Vignette · Opening a New Session
Scenario

Ryan is a consultant. His current project is a four-session AI training series for a team of staff at a mental health organisation. He's already delivered the first three sessions — and he's used Claude to help design and build all of them.

He's now sitting down to work on the final session. On his hard drive he has everything: the slide decks from all three sessions, the full transcripts from each delivery, the markdown planning documents, the activity materials, and the website he built for Session 3. It's a substantial body of work.

He opens a new Claude conversation. He's ready to get started.

What's the first thing he does — before opening any of those files?

Why might reaching for the hard drive not be the best first move?

The Memory Check

Before reaching for any of those materials, Ryan checks what Claude already knows.

"I've been working with you over the past couple of months on a four-session AI training series for QAMH — Queensland Alliance for Mental Health. Together we've designed and built all three sessions delivered so far: the slides, the activities, the planning documents, the website for Session 3. I'm now working on Session 4, the final one. Before I share anything with you today, I want you to search your memory of our previous conversations and tell me what you already know about this project — the content we've covered, the decisions we've made, the participants, the design principles we've been working with. Tell me what you have, and I'll fill in the gaps."

Claude searches its memory. It surfaces the session arc, the participant group, the key design decisions, the tone and philosophy of the training — much of it without a single file being uploaded.

The hard drive stays closed. The conversation starts from a richer position than any upload could have produced alone.

Ten seconds of checking saves ten minutes of uploading — and produces better context than a document ever could.

How you navigate the conversation

What to do when the conversation drifts — or when a better path opens.

Vignette · Deep in the Weeds
Scenario

Susanne has been working on a policy submission to the Queensland Government for the past hour. She's on her sixth round of edits on the opening paragraph — adjusting word choice, moving sentences around, trying to get the tone exactly right. The paragraph is getting better. She's about to move on to the next one.

Before moving to the next paragraph — what might be worth doing first?

Which element of the framework does this connect to — and what's the risk of not doing it?

The Zoom-Out Interrupt

Noticing when you've drifted into excessive detail and explicitly naming the need to pull back before continuing. The sunk cost of an hour's work can pull you deeper into the wrong level of resolution — unless you choose to interrupt it.

Try:

"Before we keep going — step back and read the whole document as it stands. What's working well? What's the biggest structural issue? What should I actually be focusing on right now?"
This question often surfaces that the paragraph you've been perfecting is in the wrong section entirely.
Vignette · A Better Idea Appears
Scenario

Jun has been working on a workforce data report for a funding body for the past 20 minutes. The conversation has produced a solid structure — methodology first, then findings, then recommendations. It's logical. But something feels off and Jun can't quite name it. The conversation keeps producing technically correct responses that somehow miss the point.

Jun is about to ask Claude to refine the methodology section again.

Then Claude includes a passing observation: the outcome data in section three is actually the most compelling thing in the whole document — the kind of number a funder immediately wants to see.

Jun stops.

What does Jun do with that observation?

What's the cost of ignoring it and continuing on the original path?

The Redirect on the Fly

The best direction often only becomes visible during the conversation itself. Jun doesn't owe the conversation its original structure. The pivot is free.

"Hold on — that observation about the outcome data changes everything. Let's change direction. The whole document should lead with that number. Can you help me restructure from that starting point?"

Treating the conversation as a live thinking tool rather than a production pipeline means staying alert to moments when something better appears — and being willing to take it without friction.

You never owe the conversation its original direction. The pivot is free.

Cluster 3

Shaping the collaboration

Calibrating how the model works with you — not just what it produces.

Vignette · Not What You Needed
Scenario

Annalise asks Claude to summarise the key themes from her consultation data. She receives back 1,200 words across eight dense paragraphs. The summary is thorough, accurate, and completely unusable for the purpose she had in mind.

She begins manually editing it down.

Before editing — what's the faster move?

And why would that help? Think about which element of the framework this connects to — context, iteration, or collaboration?

Output Calibration

Explicitly instructing the model on format, length, and register — and varying this deliberately as the task evolves. The model defaults to its own judgment about what a response should look like. That judgment is frequently misaligned with the current stage of work.

Try:

"I need this in a completely different format. Five bullet points maximum. Each under 25 words. Nothing else."

Or at the start: "Keep your responses brief for now — just acknowledge that you've understood. We'll expand later."

Or the opposite: "I won't limit you here — take creative freedom with this one."

Every stage of a task has a different optimal output format. Calibrate it explicitly.
Vignette · The Blank Screen
Scenario

It's late on a Friday. Sasha has a complex task ahead — a strategic plan update — but her brain is foggy and she doesn't know where to begin. She stares at the blank screen. She tries to start. Nothing comes.

What's the move when you can't generate anything yourself?

And why would that help? Think about which element of the framework this connects to — context, iteration, or collaboration?

The Cognitive Offload

When bandwidth is low, shift the generative role to the model and position yourself as the corrector and quality filter rather than the originator. This is often faster than generating from scratch regardless of how you're feeling — and it produces better output.

Try:

"I'm feeling foggy and I'm not sure where to start. You know quite a bit about my work from our previous conversations. Can you lay out what you think the main components of this strategic plan update should look like? I'll correct and redirect — I just need something to react to."
Editing is always easier than generating. Let the model generate. You edit.
Vignette · Describing the Indescribable
Scenario

Bernadette needs to write a funding acquittal report — a document type she hasn't produced before. She begins describing to Claude what she thinks it should include: the sections, the approximate length, the tone, the audience.

Claude produces something. It's fine, but it doesn't look or feel like what she had in mind.

What would have been faster than describing it?

And why would that help? Think about which element of the framework this connects to — context, iteration, or collaboration?

The Style Anchor

Rather than describing what you want in the abstract, provide an existing artifact and ask the model to use it as a baseline. Showing beats telling — and this applies just as much when instructing a model as when teaching in a classroom.

Try:

"I'm not going to describe the format. Instead, here's an example of the kind of document I want to produce. Please study the structure, the tone, the length, and the way information is organised — but ignore the content. Now, here's what I actually need to write about…"
Previous examples do more contextual work than any description. Use them.

🌻 Build Something That Fits You

The Setup

Imagine you work in an environment with extremely loose structure. You're self-directed — no one is watching the clock, and you decide what to work on and when. You also have ADHD, which means you struggle to initiate high-stakes tasks and tend to hyperfixate on lower-priority work when you should be doing something else.

You've tried Notion, calendar blocking, various productivity apps. None of them quite fit the specific shape of how your brain works. You find yourself thinking: what if I could build something that actually fits me?

You decide to try building a custom daily planner app using Claude's help. You want it to handle daily, weekly, and monthly structure, track progress across larger projects, and include features that are specifically designed for your ADHD quirks.

Step 01

You have a vision. You know roughly what you want. You open Claude.

What's the first thing you do?

Why that, rather than jumping straight into building?

The Thinking-Out-Loud Drop + Scene-Setting

You don't start building. You start thinking — out loud, with Claude as the thinking partner. Describe your brain, your quirks, your workflow, your frustrations with other tools. Get everything out before you architect anything.

"I have ADHD and I've never found a planning tool that works for my brain. Let me describe what I need and what hasn't worked before, and I want us to brainstorm what a custom tool might look like..."
The rough thing first. The model finds the shape in it.
Step 02

You've had a rich brainstorming conversation. You have pages of ideas, features, and reasoning. Before you start building anything technical, what do you do with all of this?

How do you capture and carry this thinking forward?

Why not just start building from the conversation?

The Project Handoff Document

Ask Claude to crystallise everything into a structured Markdown document: the purpose of the app, every feature with the reasoning behind it, the design principles, and a technical spec. This document travels with the project and anchors every future conversation.

"Before we build anything, I want you to produce a Markdown planning document that captures everything we've just discussed — the purpose, all the features and why each one exists, and a technical spec. This will be the foundation document for the build."
The conversation is thinking. The document is the decision. Don't skip it.
Step 03

You have your Markdown planning document. You're ready to start actually building the app. Where do you go?

Which environment do you move into, and why?

Knowing When to Switch Tools

You move into VS Code with Claude Code. The chat window is for thinking and designing. Claude Code is for building and iterating on technical work. The Markdown document goes into the project folder — Claude Code reads it and uses it as its foundation.

Different tools for different stages. Know which is which.
Step 04

You're working in Claude Code. The app is taking shape. You want to make a change — something about how the weekly view works. You know what you want in plain language but you're not sure how to describe it to Claude Code precisely.

Where do you go to work out how to describe this change?

Why not just describe it directly to Claude Code?

The Cross-Instance Translation

Go back to the normal Claude chat window. Describe what you want in plain language. Ask Claude to generate the best possible prompt to give to Claude Code. Claude knows how to talk to other instances of Claude far better than you do.

"I want to describe a change to make to my app, and I need you to translate this into the best prompt to give to Claude Code. Here's what I want in plain language: [describe it]."
You stay in natural language. Claude handles the dialect at every boundary.
Step 05

The app works locally. You want it live — accessible from any browser, shareable with anyone.

How do you get a working local app onto the internet if you don't know how to deploy things?

What's the key to making a technically unfamiliar process manageable?

The Paced Walkthrough

Ask Claude how to deploy it — but ask for instructions one step at a time. Share a screenshot of what you're actually seeing. Confirm completion before the next step is given. The compounding-error problem disappears entirely.

"I want to deploy this app. Please give me one instruction at a time, and I'll share a screenshot of what I'm seeing at each step. Wait for my confirmation before moving on."
The skill isn't knowing how to deploy. It's knowing how to ask.

The result.

A real, live, working daily planner — built specifically for one person's brain. No coding background. No technical training. Just the process you've been building all along.

Learning something new with what you already know.

Scene-Setting + Capability Consultation

Here is the actual prompt used to open the conversation:

"I'm aware of the 'Claude Skills' function but I don't know anything about how it works or how to leverage it well for my own work. My goal today is to learn about this capability and ultimately build some custom skills that best fit my unique needs. Prioritising and Extracting

Official documentation tells you how things work. Tutorial creators tell you how things work in practice — the failure modes, the non-obvious tricks, the things discovered through hours of experimentation that official docs don't mention.

That's why videos #3 and #4 are the priority. High view counts (84K and 46K) are a quality signal: thousands of people have already found them worth their time. But watching 46-minute videos is slow. The move is to extract the transcripts instead.

How to get a YouTube transcript:

  1. Open the YouTube video in your browser
  2. Below the video, find the grey description box and click the More button
  3. Scroll to the bottom of the expanded description and click Show transcript
  4. The transcript appears alongside the video on the right-hand side
  5. Click and drag to select and copy the full transcript text
  6. Paste into a plain text file and save — repeat for each video you want to use
You've just turned hours of passive watching into something a model can read in seconds.
